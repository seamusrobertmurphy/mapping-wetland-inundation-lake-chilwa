## SAR-Optical Fusion for Wetland and Inland Water Mapping

Optical water indices (NDWI, MNDWI) struggle where vegetation canopies obscure standing water, where turbidity or shallow depth weakens spectral contrast with surrounding land, and where persistent cloud cover creates temporal gaps (Gallant et al., 2015; Sogno et al., 2022). SAR penetrates cloud and, at C-band, partially penetrates vegetation canopies, but performs poorly at discriminating spectrally distinct land cover types and produces high commission errors in rough terrain or urban surfaces (Cazals et al., 2021). Fusing the two sensor families exploits their complementarity: SAR supplies all-weather structural and moisture sensitivity while optical data supply spectral discrimination.

Three fusion strategies dominate the literature. Pixel-level stacking concatenates SAR backscatter bands (typically Sentinel-1 VV and VH) with optical bands and derived indices into a single feature space for classification (Tavus et al., 2024; Fichtner et al., 2020). Decision-level fusion runs independent classifiers on each data source and combines their outputs through voting, probability averaging, or stacking ensemble models (Chatziantoniou et al., 2017; Shrestha et al., 2023). Machine learning classifiers, particularly random forest, are the most common integration framework. RF trained on combined SAR and optical features consistently outperforms single-source models, with reported overall accuracies of 85 to 95 percent for wetland classes (Amani et al., 2019; Whyte et al., 2018).

Several studies have fused Sentinel-1 with Landsat or Sentinel-2 for wetland and lake mapping. Tavus et al. (2024) combined Landsat-8/9, Sentinel-2, and Sentinel-1 for surface water monitoring, reducing median revisit intervals from over 15 days to under 5. Xu et al. (2025) mapped seamless surface water dynamics across East Africa at 10-metre resolution by integrating Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 time series from 2017 to 2023. In tropical Africa, Lubala et al. (2023) fused Sentinel-1, Sentinel-2, and ALOS-PALSAR to map small inland wetlands in South Kivu, Democratic Republic of Congo, achieving kappa exceeding 0.70 and outperforming existing regional products. Gasirabo et al. (2023) fused Sentinel-1 SAR and Sentinel-2 MSI for urban land cover in Gondar, Ethiopia, confirming gains from combined inputs.

Temporal mismatch between SAR and optical acquisitions is handled in several ways: compositing both sources over shared time windows (monthly or seasonal), using multi-temporal feature stacks that capture phenological and hydrological cycles across an entire year, or applying decision-level fusion where each sensor contributes its nearest-date classification independently (Xu et al., 2025; Shrestha et al., 2023). For fluctuating and shallow water bodies, including endorheic systems, Wang et al. (2024) demonstrated that SAR-optical fusion correctly captured seasonal trends in water extent that single-sensor methods missed, while Zhang et al. (2019) used Sentinel-1 time series to detect seasonal lake cycles on the Tibetan Plateau, a context analogous to Lake Chilwa's endorheic regime.
